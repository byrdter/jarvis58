# Video Cold-Open & Curiosity-Gap Rule (Byrddynasty / "Understanding AI")

**Scope:** every Byrddynasty long-form video and every short. Applies whenever scripting a
cold open or a hook. This is the `.claude`-surface mirror of the canonical playbook —
the full reasoning lives in
`.agents/skills/jarvis-video-production/knowledge/RETENTION-AND-HOOKS.md` (§2–§3). Read it
before scripting.

## Why this exists
Channel is **retention-gated, not quality-gated**: ~70% of viewers left in the first ~30s, so
the algorithm never distributed the videos (0% Browse/Search/External). Fix = the first 30
seconds + an ~8-minute runtime. Proven on the V6 and V5 8-min recuts.

## The cold-open template (face-first) — no exceptions
1. **0:00 FACE FIRST.** Avatar on camera from frame one delivering the hook. Never open on a
   dark abstract graphic + disembodied voice (reads as "AI slop").
2. **~0:00–0:20 THE HOOK.** The single most provocative concrete stake, framed as a **paradox**
   where possible.
3. **~0:20 THE AVATAR JOKE (one line) — make it TOPICAL.** Base line + a self-deprecating turn
   that uses *this video's subject* against the avatar. Formula: *"…he writes every word, I just
   say them. Which, for a video about [subject], is [irony]."* V1: "the irony of an AI avatar
   hosting a video about AI making things up"; V5: "for a story about a machine that produces
   fluent, confident… things that are completely made up"; V6: "machines that sound exactly as
   trustworthy when they're wrong as when they're right." The bare "I just say them" stub with no
   topical turn is the weak fallback — always tie the joke to the subject. Keep this; cut ALL other bio.
4. **~0:20–0:35 RE-OPEN THE LOOP.** End on a **named question**, not the thesis.

**DELETE FOREVER from openings:** the "38 years" bio, "on this channel we keep asking one
question," "Welcome back to Understanding AI," "today we're going to explore/talk about…" —
every line that says the video hasn't started yet. Be started.

## Three devices that sharpen the open (from competitor teardowns)
Construction moves for the HOOK / RE-OPEN steps, from the top-outlier cold opens in our set
(Internet of Bugs teardown; first applied to AI-Finance V1, 2026-07-22). Full version:
`jarvis-video-production/knowledge/RETENTION-AND-HOOKS.md` §2.5.
1. **Adversary in sentence one.** Open on a conflict with sides, not a fact or topic. The adversary
   can be a person, a consensus, a received analogy, an accounting treatment. If sentence one is a
   fact, lead instead with the claim that fact argues against. (V1: "everyone is arguing about which
   bubble this is" — not the CFO quote.)
2. **The negation ladder.** *"Not because X. Not because Y. Because ——."* Deny the two guesses the
   viewer would reach for, withhold the third. A fixed-grammar curiosity gap. (V1: "Not because it's
   hidden. Not because anyone broke a rule. Because of a decision about what counts as debt.")
3. **Decline the frame fight.** When a proven competitor video owns the obvious framing, don't argue
   inside it — name the whole argument as the wrong question and step outside. Turns their video into
   your setup; paradox-shaped. Needs existing demand (§7.3). (V1 vs. IoB's dot-com-bubble video: "we
   read the filings instead, and they say something stranger.")
> **Then cut what the new open already delivers.** Sharpening the first lines makes a later paragraph
> redundant — re-scan the next two scenes and delete it, or you've only added runtime.

## The curiosity-gap rule (the reasoning — use forever)
Hooks work by **opening gaps, not by hiding information**. Curiosity is the felt gap between
what you know and what you want to know — the viewer must know something concrete to feel it.
The test is never "how much did I reveal?" It is: **"Does the viewer now have a question they
genuinely can't answer on their own?"**

- **Reveal FACTS freely** — events, numbers, the situation. They build credibility and tension.
- **Withhold MEANING** — the explanation, mechanism, verdict, twist, resolution. That's the
  payoff they stay 8 minutes to receive.
- **Prefer a PARADOX** — it's self-sealing (an open loop the viewer can't resolve alone). More
  information that *deepens* a contradiction increases retention; more that *resolves* it kills
  it. Same words, opposite effect.
- **Boundary: REVEAL UP TO THE QUESTION, STOP BEFORE THE ANSWER.** The moment you explain the
  *why*, you've spent the video.
- **Loop-naming ≠ agenda-setting.** ✅ "how that's possible — and who decides what we do about
  it" (a cliffhanger). ❌ "today we'll look at some studies" (a table of contents).

**Two tests on any hook:**
1. *Prediction:* a smart viewer knows exactly what's at stake but **cannot predict the answer** = perfect.
2. *Fact-vs-meaning:* sort every sentence — fact/number (give) vs interpretation/verdict/mechanism (withhold).

**One line:** *Give them the locked door and let them see it's locked. Do not hand them the key.*

## Before the hook: pick the idea (§7 of the playbook)
When the topic ISN'T already chosen, run ideation first — a perfect hook on a dead concept still dies.
- **Outlier score = views ÷ the posting channel's sub count.** Rank candidates by this, NEVER by raw
  views. >5× is a candidate; weight toward channels near our size. Raw views measure the channel, not
  the idea.
- **Combination titling:** fuse TWO independently-proven concepts into one that doesn't exist yet —
  don't clone one outlier. Prefer fusions that produce a **paradox**, because that's where the §3 hook
  is born. If the fused concept can't be stated as a contradiction the viewer can't resolve alone,
  fuse a different pair before writing any VO.
- **Burning problem, not mild interest** (job / kid's education / safety / can't tell what's true).
  Low competition usually means no proven demand, not an open lane.

Full method + provenance: `RETENTION-AND-HOOKS.md` §7.

## Also standing
- **~8 min** runtime (7:30–9:00). **2 shorts per video** (distinct angles; same hook rule on
  the first second). Mid-video reversal at ~40–55%. New visual beat every 3–5s.
- Narrator is first-person **plural** (exceptions: avatar self-ID, verbatim quotes, model demo
  lines). **No video references another** — batch, not an on-screen series.
